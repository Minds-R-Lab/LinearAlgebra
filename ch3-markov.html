Markov Chains

Modeling systems that transition between states — predicting long-term behavior using stochastic matrices and eigenvalues

Learning Objectives
  • Define stochastic (Markov) matrices and state vectors
  • Model real-world processes as Markov chains
  • Compute state vectors after \(k\) steps
  • Find steady-state (equilibrium) vectors
  • Connect steady states to eigenvalue \(\lambda = 1\)
  • Understand regular Markov chains and convergence

1. Stochastic Matrices & State Vectors

A stochastic matrix (or transition matrix) is a square matrix where each column sums to 1 and all entries are non-negative. These matrices arise naturally when modeling systems that transition between discrete states with probabilistic rules.

Definition: Stochastic Matrix

A matrix \(P\) is a stochastic matrix if:

  • All entries satisfy \(P_{ij} \geq 0\)
  • Each column sums to 1: \(\sum_{i=1}^{n} P_{ij} = 1\) for all \(j\)

The entry \(P_{ij}\) represents the probability of transitioning from state \(j\) to state \(i\) in one time step.

A state vector \(\mathbf{x}\) is a column vector where each entry represents the probability of being in that state:

Definition: State Vector

A state vector \(\mathbf{x}\) satisfies:

  • \(x_i \geq 0\) for all \(i\)
  • \(\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i = 1\)

Here \(x_i\) is the probability of being in state \(i\).

Example 1: Verify a stochastic matrix ⌄
Problem: Is the following matrix stochastic? \[ P = \begin{bmatrix} 0.7 & 0.2 \\ 0.3 & 0.8 \end{bmatrix} \]
Solution:
Check that all entries are non-negative: ✓ (all entries in [0,1])
Check that columns sum to 1:
  • Column 1: \(0.7 + 0.3 = 1\) ✓
  • Column 2: \(0.2 + 0.8 = 1\) ✓
Answer: Yes, \(P\) is a stochastic matrix.
Example 2: Compute state vectors after 1 and 2 steps ⌄
Problem: Let \(P = \begin{bmatrix} 0.7 & 0.2 \\ 0.3 & 0.8 \end{bmatrix}\) and initial state \(\mathbf{x}_0 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\). Find \(\mathbf{x}_1\) and \(\mathbf{x}_2\).
Solution:
After one step: \(\mathbf{x}_1 = P\mathbf{x}_0\) \[ \mathbf{x}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 0.7 & 0.2 \\ 0.3 & 0.8 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.7 \\ 0.3 \end{bmatrix} \]
After two steps: \(\mathbf{x}_2 = P\mathbf{x}_1\) \[ \mathbf{x}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 0.7 & 0.2 \\ 0.3 & 0.8 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0.7 \\ 0.3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.7(0.7) + 0.2(0.3) \\ 0.3(0.7) + 0.8(0.3) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.55 \\ 0.45 \end{bmatrix} \] Answer: \(\mathbf{x}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 0.7 \\ 0.3 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(\mathbf{x}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 0.55 \\ 0.45 \end{bmatrix}\)

2. Markov Chain Dynamics

The core equation of a Markov chain is remarkably simple: if \(\mathbf{x}_k\) is the state vector after \(k\) steps, then:

Markov Chain Recurrence Relation
\[ \mathbf{x}_{k+1} = P \mathbf{x}_k \]

By induction, \(\mathbf{x}_k = P^k \mathbf{x}_0\), where \(P^k\) is the \(k\)-step transition matrix.

This means we can compute the state vector at any time by multiplying the initial state by powers of the transition matrix.

Example 3: Weather model — sunny/rainy transitions ⌄
Problem: The weather is modeled as: if today is sunny, tomorrow is sunny with probability 0.8 and rainy with probability 0.2. If today is rainy, tomorrow is sunny with probability 0.3 and rainy with probability 0.7. Today is sunny. What is the probability of being sunny 3 days from now?
Solution:
Transition matrix (Sunny → column 1, Rainy → column 2): \[ P = \begin{bmatrix} 0.8 & 0.3 \\ 0.2 & 0.7 \end{bmatrix} \] Initial state (sunny today): \(\mathbf{x}_0 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\)

After 1 day: \[ \mathbf{x}_1 = P\mathbf{x}_0 = \begin{bmatrix} 0.8 \\ 0.2 \end{bmatrix} \]
After 2 days: \[ \mathbf{x}_2 = P\mathbf{x}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 0.8 & 0.3 \\ 0.2 & 0.7 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 0.8 \\ 0.2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.64 + 0.06 \\ 0.16 + 0.14 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.70 \\ 0.30 \end{bmatrix} \]
After 3 days: \[ \mathbf{x}_3 = P\mathbf{x}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 0.8 & 0.3 \\ 0.2 & 0.7 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 0.70 \\ 0.30 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.56 + 0.09 \\ 0.14 + 0.21 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.65 \\ 0.35 \end{bmatrix} \] Answer: The probability of sunny weather 3 days from now is \(0.65\) or 65%.
Example 4: Market share — two competing brands ⌄
Problem: Brand A currently has 60% market share, Brand B has 40%. Each quarter: customers of A stay with A 85% of the time, 15% switch to B. Customers of B stay with B 80% of the time, 20% switch to A. What is the market share after 2 quarters?
Solution:
Transition matrix: \[ P = \begin{bmatrix} 0.85 & 0.20 \\ 0.15 & 0.80 \end{bmatrix} \] Initial state: \(\mathbf{x}_0 = \begin{bmatrix} 0.60 \\ 0.40 \end{bmatrix}\)

After 1 quarter: \[ \mathbf{x}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 0.85 & 0.20 \\ 0.15 & 0.80 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 0.60 \\ 0.40 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.51 + 0.08 \\ 0.09 + 0.32 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.59 \\ 0.41 \end{bmatrix} \]
After 2 quarters: \[ \mathbf{x}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 0.85 & 0.20 \\ 0.15 & 0.80 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 0.59 \\ 0.41 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.5015 + 0.082 \\ 0.0885 + 0.328 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.5835 \\ 0.4165 \end{bmatrix} \] Answer: After 2 quarters: Brand A has ~58.35% market share, Brand B has ~41.65%.
Example 5: Three-state commute model ⌄
Problem: A commuter can go by car (C), bus (B), or bike (K). Transition probabilities each day:
From C: 0.5 stay, 0.3 to B, 0.2 to K
From B: 0.2 to C, 0.6 stay, 0.2 to K
From K: 0.3 to C, 0.4 to B, 0.3 stay
If today is car, find the distribution after 1 day.
Solution:
Transition matrix (columns: C, B, K): \[ P = \begin{bmatrix} 0.5 & 0.2 & 0.3 \\ 0.3 & 0.6 & 0.4 \\ 0.2 & 0.2 & 0.3 \end{bmatrix} \] Initial state: \(\mathbf{x}_0 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\)

After 1 day: \[ \mathbf{x}_1 = P\mathbf{x}_0 = \begin{bmatrix} 0.5 \\ 0.3 \\ 0.2 \end{bmatrix} \] Answer: Tomorrow: 50% car, 30% bus, 20% bike.

3. Steady-State Vectors

A fundamental question: does a Markov chain converge to a long-term distribution? The answer involves finding the steady-state vector.

Definition: Steady-State Vector

A vector \(\mathbf{q}\) is a steady-state vector (or equilibrium distribution) if:

\[ P\mathbf{q} = \mathbf{q} \]

This means \(\mathbf{q}\) is an eigenvector of \(P\) with eigenvalue \(\lambda = 1\).

To find the steady-state vector, we solve \((P - I)\mathbf{q} = \mathbf{0}\), which is a homogeneous system. The solutions lie in the null space of \(P - I\).

Example 6: Find steady-state for 2×2 stochastic matrix ⌄
Problem: For \(P = \begin{bmatrix} 0.7 & 0.2 \\ 0.3 & 0.8 \end{bmatrix}\), find the steady-state vector.
Solution:
We need \(P\mathbf{q} = \mathbf{q}\), or \((P - I)\mathbf{q} = \mathbf{0}\). \[ P - I = \begin{bmatrix} -0.3 & 0.2 \\ 0.3 & -0.2 \end{bmatrix} \]
Reduce: Row 1 is \(-0.3q_1 + 0.2q_2 = 0\), so \(0.3q_1 = 0.2q_2\), giving \(q_2 = 1.5q_1\).

With constraint \(q_1 + q_2 = 1\): \[ q_1 + 1.5q_1 = 1 \implies 2.5q_1 = 1 \implies q_1 = 0.4 \]
Thus \(q_2 = 0.6\).

Answer: \(\mathbf{q} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.4 \\ 0.6 \end{bmatrix}\)
Verification: \(P\mathbf{q} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.7(0.4) + 0.2(0.6) \\ 0.3(0.4) + 0.8(0.6) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.4 \\ 0.6 \end{bmatrix}\) ✓
Example 7: Weather steady state ⌄
Problem: For the weather model \(P = \begin{bmatrix} 0.8 & 0.3 \\ 0.2 & 0.7 \end{bmatrix}\), find the long-term probability distribution.
Solution: \[ P - I = \begin{bmatrix} -0.2 & 0.3 \\ 0.2 & -0.3 \end{bmatrix} \] Row 1: \(-0.2q_1 + 0.3q_2 = 0 \implies q_1 = 1.5q_2\)

With \(q_1 + q_2 = 1\): \[ 1.5q_2 + q_2 = 1 \implies 2.5q_2 = 1 \implies q_2 = 0.4 \]
Thus \(q_1 = 0.6\).

Answer: Long-term: 60% sunny, 40% rainy.
Example 8: Stochastic matrices always have eigenvalue 1 ⌄
Problem: Prove that every stochastic matrix has eigenvalue \(\lambda = 1\).
Solution:
Let \(\mathbf{e} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ \vdots \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\) (the all-ones vector).

For a stochastic matrix \(P\), each column sums to 1. Thus: \[ P\mathbf{e} = \begin{bmatrix} \text{sum of column 1} \\ \text{sum of column 2} \\ \vdots \\ \text{sum of column n} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ \vdots \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \mathbf{e} \]
Therefore \(\mathbf{e}\) is an eigenvector with eigenvalue \(\lambda = 1\). This guarantees every stochastic matrix has \(\lambda = 1\).

4. Regular Markov Chains

Not all Markov chains converge to a unique steady state. Regular Markov chains are guaranteed to converge.

Definition: Regular Markov Chain

A stochastic matrix \(P\) is regular if some power \(P^k\) has all positive entries (strictly positive).

Key Theorem: If \(P\) is regular, then:

  • \(P\) has a unique steady-state vector \(\mathbf{q}\) with all positive entries
  • For any initial state \(\mathbf{x}_0\), \(P^k\mathbf{x}_0 \to \mathbf{q}\) as \(k \to \infty\)

Example 9: Identify regular Markov chain ⌄
Problem: Is \(P = \begin{bmatrix} 0.7 & 0.2 \\ 0.3 & 0.8 \end{bmatrix}\) regular?
Solution:
\(P\) itself has all positive entries, so \(P^1 = P\) has all positive entries.
Answer: Yes, \(P\) is regular. The steady-state \(\mathbf{q} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.4 \\ 0.6 \end{bmatrix}\) is unique and guaranteed to be the limit.
Example 10: Compute powers using diagonalization ⌄
Problem: For \(P = \begin{bmatrix} 0.7 & 0.2 \\ 0.3 & 0.8 \end{bmatrix}\), find \(P^{10}\).
Solution:
Characteristic polynomial: \(\det(P - \lambda I) = (0.7-\lambda)(0.8-\lambda) - 0.06 = \lambda^2 - 1.5\lambda + 0.5 = 0\)
Eigenvalues: \(\lambda_1 = 1\), \(\lambda_2 = 0.5\)

For \(\lambda_1 = 1\): eigenvector \(\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}\) (or normalized)
For \(\lambda_2 = 0.5\): eigenvector \(\mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}\)

\(P = QDQ^{-1}\) where \(D = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0.5 \end{bmatrix}\)

\(P^{10} = QD^{10}Q^{-1} \approx \begin{bmatrix} 0.4 & 0.4 \\ 0.6 & 0.6 \end{bmatrix}\) (converges to steady-state as the 0.5 term vanishes)
Answer: \(P^{10}\) is very close to the steady-state matrix, with each column equal to \(\begin{bmatrix} 0.4 \\ 0.6 \end{bmatrix}\).

5. Applications of Markov Chains

Markov chains model phenomena across science, engineering, and economics. Here are key applications:

Example 11: Internet browsing model ⌄
Problem: Three websites: A, B, C. Transition probabilities:
From A: 0.5 stay, 0.3 to B, 0.2 to C
From B: 0.2 to A, 0.4 stay, 0.4 to C
From C: 0.3 to A, 0.3 to B, 0.4 stay
Find the steady-state distribution (fraction of time spent on each site).
Solution: \[ P = \begin{bmatrix} 0.5 & 0.2 & 0.3 \\ 0.3 & 0.4 & 0.3 \\ 0.2 & 0.4 & 0.4 \end{bmatrix} \]
Solve \((P-I)\mathbf{q} = \mathbf{0}\): \[ \begin{bmatrix} -0.5 & 0.2 & 0.3 \\ 0.3 & -0.6 & 0.3 \\ 0.2 & 0.4 & -0.6 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} q_1 \\ q_2 \\ q_3 \end{bmatrix} = \mathbf{0} \]
From Row 1: \(0.5q_1 = 0.2q_2 + 0.3q_3\)
From Row 2: \(0.3q_1 + 0.6q_2 = 0.3q_3\)
With \(q_1 + q_2 + q_3 = 1\), solving yields approximately: \[ \mathbf{q} \approx \begin{bmatrix} 0.36 \\ 0.32 \\ 0.32 \end{bmatrix} \] Answer: Long-term, about 36% on A, 32% on B, 32% on C.
Example 12: Gambler's ruin (absorbing chain) ⌄
Problem: A gambler with \$2 plays a fair game. Each round: win \$1 with probability 0.5, lose \$1 with probability 0.5. Game ends at \$0 (ruin) or \$4 (victory). What is the probability of reaching \$4?
Solution:
States: 0, 1, 2, 3, 4 (where 0 and 4 are absorbing) \[ P = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0.5 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0.5 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0.5 & 0 & 0.5 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0.5 & 0 & 0.5 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0.5 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \]
Let \(p_i\) = probability of reaching \$4 starting from \$\(i\). Boundary: \(p_0 = 0\), \(p_4 = 1\).
Recurrence for fair game: \(p_i = 0.5 p_{i-1} + 0.5 p_{i+1}\), which gives \(p_i = i/4\).
Starting from \$2: \(p_2 = 2/4 = 0.5\)
Answer: The gambler has a 50% chance of reaching \$4 (and 50% of ruin).
Example 13: Google PageRank (simplified) ⌄
Problem: Four webpages: A, B, C, D. Links:
A links to B, C
B links to A
C links to A, B, D
D links to A, C
(Assume random surfer follows each link with equal probability.)
Find the steady-state PageRank.
Solution:
Transition matrix (out-degree normalized):
From A (2 links): to B (0.5), to C (0.5)
From B (1 link): to A (1.0)
From C (3 links): to A (1/3), to B (1/3), to D (1/3)
From D (2 links): to A (0.5), to C (0.5) \[ P = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 1/3 & 0.5 \\ 0.5 & 0 & 1/3 & 0 \\ 0.5 & 0 & 0 & 0.5 \\ 0 & 0 & 1/3 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \]
Solving \(P\mathbf{q} = \mathbf{q}\) (approximately): \[ \mathbf{q} \approx \begin{bmatrix} 0.40 \\ 0.20 \\ 0.20 \\ 0.20 \end{bmatrix} \]
Answer: Page A has the highest PageRank (0.40); others share equally (0.20 each).
Example 14: Genetics — gene frequency evolution ⌄
Problem: A gene has two alleles: A and a. In a population, frequency of AA is 0.25, Aa is 0.5, aa is 0.25. Under random mating, what is the frequency after one generation?
Solution:
Allele frequencies: \(p(A) = 0.25 + 0.25 = 0.5\), \(p(a) = 0.25 + 0.25 = 0.5\)
Under Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium:
AA: \(p^2 = 0.25\)
Aa: \(2pq = 0.5\)
aa: \(q^2 = 0.25\)
Since we started at equilibrium, these frequencies persist.
Answer: Frequencies remain at (0.25, 0.5, 0.25) — the population is in equilibrium.
Example 15: Regular vs non-regular chains ⌄
Problem: Determine if \(P_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(P_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 0.5 & 0.3 \\ 0.5 & 0.7 \end{bmatrix}\) are regular.
Solution:
\(P_1\): \(P_1^2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\) (identity). Since \(P_1\) has zeros, it's not regular. It oscillates: odd powers are \(P_1\), even powers are \(I\).

\(P_2\): \(P_2\) itself has all positive entries, so it's regular. It converges to a unique steady state.

Answer: \(P_1\) is non-regular (periodic); \(P_2\) is regular (aperiodic).
