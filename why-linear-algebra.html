Why Study Linear Algebra?

The mathematical language of data, space, and transformation — and the hidden engine behind modern technology

The Language of Structure

Look at the world through a different lens. Every image on your screen is a matrix of pixel values. Every search query you type triggers a massive matrix multiplication. Every 3D video game renders frames by multiplying transformation matrices sixty times per second. Linear algebra is the mathematics of organized information — and organized information is the foundation of modern civilization.

While calculus studies change, linear algebra studies structure: how data is arranged, how spaces are shaped, how transformations move objects from one form to another. It answers questions like:

What is Linear Algebra, Exactly?

At its core, linear algebra is the study of vectors, matrices, and linear transformations. But what does "linear" really mean?

Definition — Linearity

A function \(T\) is linear if it satisfies two properties for all vectors \(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}\) and scalar \(c\):

$$T(\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}) = T(\mathbf{u}) + T(\mathbf{v}) \qquad \text{(additivity)}$$
$$T(c\,\mathbf{u}) = c\,T(\mathbf{u}) \qquad \text{(homogeneity)}$$

These two properties together mean: the function preserves addition and scaling. Remarkably, every such function can be represented as multiplication by a matrix.

The Three Fundamental Objects

➡

Vectors

Ordered lists of numbers representing points, directions, states, or data.

\(\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -1 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}\)
A single data point in \(\mathbb{R}^n\)
▦

Matrices

Rectangular arrays of numbers — they encode systems of equations, transformations, and data.

\(A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 3 \end{pmatrix}\)
A linear transformation or a data table
↺

Linear Transformations

Functions that map vectors to vectors while preserving linearity.

\(T(\mathbf{x}) = A\mathbf{x}\)
Every linear map is a matrix multiply

The Central Problem: Solving Ax = b

If there is one equation that summarizes the entire course, it is this:

$$A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$$

Given a matrix \(A\) and a vector \(\mathbf{b}\), find the vector \(\mathbf{x}\) (if it exists). This single equation encodes:

InterpretationWhat \(A\), \(\mathbf{x}\), \(\mathbf{b}\) represent
System of equationsCoefficients, unknowns, right-hand side
Linear transformationTransformation, input, output
Data fittingDesign matrix, parameters, observations
Network flowIncidence matrix, flows, demands
Machine learningFeature matrix, weights, predictions
Graph theoryAdjacency matrix, ranking scores, constraints

The remarkable thing is that one set of tools — Gaussian elimination, matrix factorizations, eigenvalue decomposition — solves all of these problems. That is the power and elegance of linear algebra.

Quick Example: Three Equations, Three Unknowns

Consider the system:

$$\begin{cases} 2x + y - z = 8 \\ -3x - y + 2z = -11 \\ -2x + y + 2z = -3 \end{cases}$$

In matrix form, this is \(A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}\) with:

$$\underbrace{\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & -1 \\ -3 & -1 & 2 \\ -2 & 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}}_{A} \underbrace{\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{pmatrix}}_{\mathbf{x}} = \underbrace{\begin{pmatrix} 8 \\ -11 \\ -3 \end{pmatrix}}_{\mathbf{b}}$$

Gaussian elimination (which we will study in Chapter 1) gives us \(x = 2, \; y = 3, \; z = -1\). One algorithm, one framework — works whether the system has 3 variables or 3 million.

See It: Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors

Most vectors change direction when multiplied by a matrix. But special vectors called eigenvectors keep their direction — they only get stretched or shrunk by a factor \(\lambda\) (the eigenvalue). This is perhaps the most powerful concept in all of linear algebra.

Definition — Eigenvalue Equation

\(A\mathbf{v} = \lambda\mathbf{v}\), where \(\mathbf{v} \neq \mathbf{0}\). The scalar \(\lambda\) is an eigenvalue and \(\mathbf{v}\) is the corresponding eigenvector.

The teal and purple vectors (eigenvectors) keep their direction when transformed — only their length changes. The yellow vector changes direction entirely. Eigenvectors reveal a matrix's natural axes of action.

Eigenvalues tell us:

|\(\lambda\)| > 1 → Stretching

The system amplifies along this direction. In a vibrating system, this means resonance or instability.

|\(\lambda\)| < 1 → Shrinking

The system contracts along this direction. In dynamics, this means decay toward equilibrium.

A Brief History of Linear Algebra

Linear algebra's roots stretch back millennia, but it became the powerhouse it is today through centuries of mathematical development:

~200 BCE — China
The Nine Chapters on the Mathematical Art describes solving systems of linear equations using a method equivalent to Gaussian elimination — nearly 2000 years before Gauss.
~800 CE — Islamic Golden Age
Al-Khwarizmi (from whose name we get "algorithm") develops systematic methods for solving linear and quadratic equations. The algebraic thinking that would later underpin matrix theory traces directly to this era.
1683 — Seki Takakazu (Japan)
Independently develops the concept of determinants for solving systems of equations, around the same time as Leibniz in Europe.
1750 — Gabriel Cramer
Publishes Cramer's Rule, giving an explicit formula for solving \(n \times n\) systems using determinants.
1810 — Carl Friedrich Gauss
Formalizes Gaussian elimination while working on astronomical calculations. The method becomes the standard algorithm for solving linear systems.
1855 — Arthur Cayley
Introduces matrix algebra as a formal mathematical system — the first person to treat matrices as objects in their own right with operations like multiplication.
1904 — David Hilbert
Develops the theory of infinite-dimensional vector spaces (Hilbert spaces), laying the mathematical foundation for quantum mechanics.
1929 — John von Neumann
Rigorously axiomatizes quantum mechanics using linear algebra. Observables become matrices, states become vectors — the language of linear algebra becomes the language of physics.
1965 — Gene Golub & William Kahan
Develop practical algorithms for the Singular Value Decomposition (SVD), which becomes one of the most important computational tools in data science and engineering.
1998 — Larry Page & Sergey Brin
Google's PageRank algorithm uses the dominant eigenvector of a massive web-link matrix to rank web pages — linear algebra literally powers the internet's search engine.
2012–present — Deep Learning Revolution
Neural networks are sequences of matrix multiplications + nonlinearities. GPUs (originally for graphics matrix math) become AI accelerators. Linear algebra is the computational backbone of modern AI.
"The theory of matrices is one of the most powerful tools in mathematics and its applications. I cannot overemphasize the importance of matrices in modern applied mathematics."
— Richard Bellman, pioneer of dynamic programming

Applications: Where Linear Algebra Powers the World

Linear algebra isn't just abstract theory — it is the working engine behind technology you use every day. Here are the major domains, with special focus on computer science applications:

Computer Science & AI

🤖

Machine Learning & AI

Every neural network forward pass is a chain of matrix multiplications. Training uses gradient descent — which computes derivatives of matrix expressions. PCA reduces high-dimensional data to its most informative axes via eigenvalue decomposition.

\(\hat{y} = \sigma(W_2 \cdot \sigma(W_1 \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b}_1) + \mathbf{b}_2)\)
🎨

Computer Graphics

Every rotation, translation, scaling, and projection in 3D graphics is a matrix multiplication. Game engines perform millions of these per frame. Homogeneous coordinates let us combine all transforms into a single \(4 \times 4\) matrix.

\(\mathbf{p}' = M_{\text{proj}} \cdot M_{\text{view}} \cdot M_{\text{model}} \cdot \mathbf{p}\)
🔍

Search Engines (PageRank)

Google ranks web pages by computing the dominant eigenvector of the web's link matrix. A page is important if important pages link to it — a self-referential definition solved elegantly by eigenvalue theory.

\(\mathbf{r} = M\mathbf{r} \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad M\mathbf{r} = 1 \cdot \mathbf{r}\)
📊

Data Science & Recommender Systems

Netflix recommendations use SVD to factorize the user-movie ratings matrix into lower-rank components, predicting what you'll watch next. The same idea powers Amazon, Spotify, and YouTube.

\(R \approx U \Sigma V^T \quad \text{(low-rank approx.)}\)
📷

Image Compression & Processing

JPEG 2000 and many compression algorithms use SVD or wavelets (which are matrix transforms) to compress images. Keeping only the top singular values preserves the image while dramatically reducing file size.

\(A_k = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \sigma_i \mathbf{u}_i \mathbf{v}_i^T\)
🔒

Cryptography

Public key cryptography, lattice-based cryptography (post-quantum), and error-correcting codes all rely heavily on linear algebra over finite fields. The Hill cipher directly encrypts using matrix multiplication.

\(\mathbf{c} = A\mathbf{p} \pmod{26}\)

Engineering & Science

🔌

Signal Processing

The Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) is a matrix multiplication. Filters are convolution matrices. Every audio, radio, and telecommunications system relies on linear algebra.

\(\mathbf{X} = F_N \mathbf{x} \quad \text{(DFT matrix)}\)
⚙

Control Systems

State-space models represent dynamic systems as \(\dot{\mathbf{x}} = A\mathbf{x} + B\mathbf{u}\). Stability depends on eigenvalues of \(A\). Controllers are designed using matrix algebra.

\(\dot{\mathbf{x}} = A\mathbf{x} + B\mathbf{u}, \quad \mathbf{y} = C\mathbf{x}\)
🏗

Structural Engineering

The finite element method discretizes structures into elements connected at nodes. The stiffness matrix \(K\) relates forces to displacements: \(K\mathbf{d} = \mathbf{f}\). Linear algebra solves for deflections.

\(K\mathbf{d} = \mathbf{f} \quad \text{(stiffness eq.)}\)
⚛

Quantum Computing

Quantum states are vectors in a Hilbert space. Quantum gates are unitary matrices. Measurement is a projection. The entire framework of quantum computing is linear algebra.

\(|\psi'\rangle = U |\psi\rangle \quad \text{(unitary gate)}\)

See It: Image Compression with SVD

One of the most striking applications of linear algebra is using the Singular Value Decomposition to compress images. The idea: any \(m \times n\) matrix (image) can be written as a sum of rank-1 matrices. Keeping only the first \(k\) terms gives an approximation that captures most of the image with far less data.

$$A = U \Sigma V^T = \sigma_1 \mathbf{u}_1 \mathbf{v}_1^T + \sigma_2 \mathbf{u}_2 \mathbf{v}_2^T + \cdots + \sigma_r \mathbf{u}_r \mathbf{v}_r^T$$
k = 5 | Compression: 90%

Drag the slider to see how many rank-1 components are needed to reconstruct the image. Low \(k\) = more compression, less detail. Higher \(k\) = less compression, more detail.

Why This Matters

A \(100 \times 100\) image stores 10,000 numbers. With rank-\(k\) SVD, you only store \(k(100 + 100 + 1) = 201k\) numbers. At \(k = 10\), that's 2,010 numbers — 80% compression while retaining recognizable quality. This principle underlies image compression, noise reduction, and dimensionality reduction across science and engineering.
