Complex Eigenvalues

When the characteristic equation has complex roots — rotation-dilation behavior and the real canonical form

Learning Objectives

  • Identify when eigenvalues are complex (negative discriminant)
  • Find complex eigenvalue pairs \(\lambda = a \pm bi\)
  • Compute complex eigenvectors
  • Understand rotation-dilation geometric interpretation
  • Write the real-valued general solution using real and imaginary parts
  • Construct the rotation-scaling matrix \(C = \begin{bmatrix} a & -b \\ b & a \end{bmatrix}\)

1. Complex Roots of the Characteristic Equation

When a real matrix has a characteristic polynomial with negative discriminant, we obtain complex eigenvalues. For a 2×2 matrix \(\begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix}\), the characteristic equation is:

\[\lambda^2 - (a+d)\lambda + (ad-bc) = 0\]

The discriminant is \(\Delta = (a+d)^2 - 4(ad-bc) = (a-d)^2 + 4bc\). When \(\Delta < 0\), the eigenvalues are complex conjugates:

Theorem: Complex Eigenvalue Pairs

If \(A\) is a real \(2 \times 2\) matrix with characteristic polynomial \(\lambda^2 - (a+d)\lambda + (ad-bc) = 0\) and negative discriminant, then the eigenvalues are:

\[\lambda_1 = \alpha + \beta i, \quad \lambda_2 = \alpha - \beta i\]

where \(\alpha = \frac{a+d}{2}\) and \(\beta = \frac{\sqrt{4(ad-bc)-(a-d)^2}}{2}\).

Example 1.1 (Easy): Pure Rotation Matrix ▼

Problem: Find the complex eigenvalues of \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}\).

Solution:

The characteristic polynomial is:

\[\det(\lambda I - A) = \det\begin{bmatrix} \lambda & 1 \\ -1 & \lambda \end{bmatrix} = \lambda^2 + 1\]

Setting equal to zero: \(\lambda^2 + 1 = 0 \Rightarrow \lambda = \pm i\)

Answer: The eigenvalues are \(\lambda_1 = i\) and \(\lambda_2 = -i\) (pure imaginary, representing a 90° rotation).

Example 1.2 (Easy): Verify Complex Eigenvalues ▼

Problem: Find the eigenvalues of \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -2 \\ 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\) and verify they are complex.

Solution:

The characteristic polynomial is:

\[\det(\lambda I - A) = \det\begin{bmatrix} \lambda - 1 & 2 \\ -1 & \lambda - 1 \end{bmatrix} = (\lambda-1)^2 + 2 = \lambda^2 - 2\lambda + 3\]

Using the quadratic formula:

\[\lambda = \frac{2 \pm \sqrt{4-12}}{2} = \frac{2 \pm \sqrt{-8}}{2} = \frac{2 \pm 2i\sqrt{2}}{2} = 1 \pm i\sqrt{2}\]

Answer: The eigenvalues are \(\lambda_1 = 1 + i\sqrt{2}\) and \(\lambda_2 = 1 - i\sqrt{2}\) (complex conjugate pair).

Example 1.3 (Easy): Verify an Eigenvalue ▼

Problem: Verify that \(\lambda = 2 + 3i\) is an eigenvalue of \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & -3 \\ 3 & 2 \end{bmatrix}\).

Solution:

We check if \(\det(\lambda I - A) = 0\):

\[\det\begin{bmatrix} (2+3i) - 2 & 3 \\ -3 & (2+3i) - 2 \end{bmatrix} = \det\begin{bmatrix} 3i & 3 \\ -3 & 3i \end{bmatrix}\]

\[= (3i)(3i) - (3)(-3) = 9i^2 + 9 = -9 + 9 = 0 \checkmark\]

Answer: Verified. \(\lambda = 2 + 3i\) is indeed an eigenvalue.

2. Complex Eigenvectors

To find a complex eigenvector \(\mathbf{v}\) for a complex eigenvalue \(\lambda = \alpha + \beta i\), we solve \((A - \lambda I)\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}\) using complex arithmetic.

Complex Eigenvector

For a complex eigenvalue \(\lambda\) of a real matrix \(A\), a complex eigenvector \(\mathbf{v}\) is a nonzero vector such that \(A\mathbf{v} = \lambda \mathbf{v}\).

Example 2.1 (Medium): Find Complex Eigenvectors ▼

Problem: Find a complex eigenvector for \(\lambda = 1 + i\) of the matrix \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -2 \\ 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\).

Solution:

We solve \((A - \lambda I)\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}\):

\[\begin{bmatrix} 1-(1+i) & -2 \\ 1 & 1-(1+i) \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -i & -2 \\ 1 & -i \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\]

From the first row: \(-i v_1 - 2v_2 = 0 \Rightarrow v_1 = \frac{-2v_2}{-i} = \frac{2v_2 \cdot i}{1} = 2iv_2\)

Let \(v_2 = 1\). Then \(v_1 = 2i\).

Answer: An eigenvector is \(\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 2i \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\).

Example 2.2 (Medium): Real Solution from Complex Eigenvector ▼

Problem: Given \(\lambda = 1 + i\) and eigenvector \(\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 2i \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\), write the real-valued general solution to \(\mathbf{x}_k = A^k \mathbf{x}_0\).

Solution:

The complex solution is \(\mathbf{x}_k^{(c)} = (1+i)^k \begin{bmatrix} 2i \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\).

To extract real solutions, we separate \(\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 2i \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} + i\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\).

So \(\text{Re}(\mathbf{v}) = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(\text{Im}(\mathbf{v}) = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\).

With \(\lambda = r e^{i\theta}\) where \(r = |1+i| = \sqrt{2}\) and \(\theta = \arctan(1/1) = \pi/4\):

\[\mathbf{x}_k = r^k \left[c_1 \cos(k\theta) \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} - c_2 \sin(k\theta) \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + c_1 \sin(k\theta) \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + c_2 \cos(k\theta) \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\right]\]

Answer: The general solution combines rotations scaled by \((\sqrt{2})^k\) and rotating by multiples of \(\pi/4\) radians per step.

3. The Rotation-Dilation Interpretation

Complex eigenvalues \(\lambda = a + bi\) represent a combination of rotation and scaling. The modulus \(r = |\lambda| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}\) determines the scaling factor, and the argument \(\theta = \arg(\lambda) = \arctan(b/a)\) determines the rotation angle.

Rotation-Dilation Interpretation

For \(\lambda = a + bi\) with \(r = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}\) and \(\theta = \arg(\lambda)\):

\[\lambda = r e^{i\theta} = r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)\]

In the discrete iteration \(\mathbf{x}_{k+1} = A\mathbf{x}_k\), the eigenvalue causes:

  • Rotation: by angle \(\theta\) each step
  • Scaling: by factor \(r\) each step
Example 3.1 (Medium): Identify Rotation and Scaling ▼

Problem: For \(\lambda = 1 + i\), find the modulus \(r\) and argument \(\theta\).

Solution:

The modulus is:

\[r = |1 + i| = \sqrt{1^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{2}\]

The argument is:

\[\theta = \arctan\left(\frac{1}{1}\right) = \arctan(1) = \frac{\pi}{4} \text{ (45 degrees)}\]

Answer: The system scales by \(\sqrt{2}\) and rotates by \(\pi/4\) radians (45°) each step.

Example 3.2 (Medium): Pure Rotation ▼

Problem: For \(\lambda = i\) (from the pure rotation matrix \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}\)), describe the geometric action.

Solution:

The modulus is \(r = |i| = 1\) (no scaling).

The argument is \(\theta = \arg(i) = \pi/2\) (90 degrees).

Answer: The system rotates by 90° each step without any scaling (pure rotation).

4. Real Canonical Form

Instead of working with complex eigenvalues, we can construct a real matrix form that captures the rotation-scaling behavior. For complex conjugate eigenvalues \(\lambda = a \pm bi\), the real canonical form is:

Real Canonical Form

If \(A\) is a real \(2 \times 2\) matrix with complex eigenvalues \(\lambda = a \pm bi\), then \(A = PCP^{-1}\) where:

\[C = \begin{bmatrix} a & -b \\ b & a \end{bmatrix}\]

The matrix \(C\) represents combined rotation (by \(\theta = \arctan(b/a)\)) and scaling (by \(r = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}\)).

Example 4.1 (Medium): Construct Real Canonical Form ▼

Problem: For matrix \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -2 \\ 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\) with eigenvalues \(\lambda = 1 \pm i\sqrt{2}\), construct the matrix \(C\).

Solution:

We have \(a = 1\) and \(b = \sqrt{2}\). Therefore:

\[C = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -\sqrt{2} \\ \sqrt{2} & 1 \end{bmatrix}\]

We can verify: modulus \(r = \sqrt{1 + 2} = \sqrt{3}\), angle \(\theta = \arctan(\sqrt{2}/1) \approx 54.7°\).

Answer: The real canonical form matrix is \(C = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -\sqrt{2} \\ \sqrt{2} & 1 \end{bmatrix}\).

Example 4.2 (Medium): Pure Rotation in Canonical Form ▼

Problem: Find the real canonical form for eigenvalues \(\lambda = \pm i\).

Solution:

For \(\lambda = 0 \pm 1 \cdot i\), we have \(a = 0\) and \(b = 1\):

\[C = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}\]

This is exactly the rotation matrix! It rotates vectors by 90° without scaling.

Answer: \(C = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}\) (standard 90° rotation matrix).

Example 4.3 (Medium): Spiral Behavior Classification ▼

Problem: Determine if the following systems produce inward or outward spirals:

(a) \(\lambda = 0.5 + 0.5i\)    (b) \(\lambda = 1.5 + i\)    (c) \(\lambda = 0.7 + 0.2i\)

Solution:

(a) \(|\lambda| = \sqrt{0.5^2 + 0.5^2} = \sqrt{0.5} \approx 0.707 < 1\) → Inward spiral

(b) \(|\lambda| = \sqrt{1.5^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{3.25} \approx 1.803 > 1\) → Outward spiral

(c) \(|\lambda| = \sqrt{0.7^2 + 0.2^2} = \sqrt{0.53} \approx 0.728 < 1\) → Inward spiral

Answer: (a) and (c) converge to origin; (b) diverges.

5. Applications and Stability Analysis

Complex eigenvalues are crucial in modeling oscillations, rotating systems, and AC circuits. The modulus determines stability: if \(|\lambda| < 1\), the system converges; if \(|\lambda| > 1\), it diverges.

Example 5.1 (Hard): 3×3 Matrix with Mixed Eigenvalues ▼

Problem: Find the eigenvalues of \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & -2 \\ 0 & 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\).

Solution:

The characteristic polynomial is:

\[\det(\lambda I - A) = \det\begin{bmatrix} \lambda-2 & -1 & 0 \\ 0 & \lambda-1 & 2 \\ 0 & -2 & \lambda-1 \end{bmatrix}\]

Expanding along the first column:

\[= (\lambda-2)\det\begin{bmatrix} \lambda-1 & 2 \\ -2 & \lambda-1 \end{bmatrix} = (\lambda-2)[(\lambda-1)^2 + 4]\]

\[= (\lambda-2)(\lambda^2 - 2\lambda + 5)\]

From \(\lambda^2 - 2\lambda + 5 = 0\): \(\lambda = \frac{2 \pm \sqrt{4-20}}{2} = 1 \pm 2i\)

Answer: The eigenvalues are \(\lambda_1 = 2\), \(\lambda_2 = 1 + 2i\), \(\lambda_3 = 1 - 2i\).

Example 5.2 (Hard): AC Circuit with Complex Impedance ▼

Problem: An RLC circuit is modeled by \(\mathbf{x}_{k+1} = A\mathbf{x}_k\) where \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 0.9 & -0.1 \\ 0.1 & 0.9 \end{bmatrix}\). Find the eigenvalues and interpret the oscillatory behavior.

Solution:

Characteristic polynomial: \(\det(\lambda I - A) = (\lambda - 0.9)^2 + 0.01 = \lambda^2 - 1.8\lambda + 0.82\)

Using the quadratic formula:

\[\lambda = \frac{1.8 \pm \sqrt{3.24 - 3.28}}{2} = \frac{1.8 \pm \sqrt{-0.04}}{2} = \frac{1.8 \pm 0.2i}{2} = 0.9 \pm 0.1i\]

Modulus: \(r = \sqrt{0.81 + 0.01} = \sqrt{0.82} \approx 0.906 < 1\) (damped oscillation)

Argument: \(\theta = \arctan(0.1/0.9) \approx 0.111 \text{ rad} \approx 6.3°\)

Answer: The circuit exhibits damped oscillations with period \(2\pi/\theta \approx 56\) steps, amplitude decreasing by factor 0.906 per step.

Example 5.3 (Hard): Stability Analysis ▼

Problem: Determine the stability of the system with eigenvalues:

(a) \(\lambda = 0.5 \pm 0.5i\)    (b) \(\lambda = 1.1 \pm 0.8i\)    (c) \(\lambda = 0.99 \pm 0.1i\)

Solution:

(a) \(|\lambda| = \sqrt{0.25 + 0.25} = \sqrt{0.5} \approx 0.707 < 1\) → Asymptotically stable (converges to origin)

(b) \(|\lambda| = \sqrt{1.21 + 0.64} = \sqrt{1.85} \approx 1.36 > 1\) → Unstable (diverges)

(c) \(|\lambda| = \sqrt{0.9801 + 0.01} = \sqrt{0.9901} \approx 0.995 < 1\) → Asymptotically stable (very slow convergence)

Answer: (a) and (c) are stable; (b) is unstable.

Example 5.4 (Hard): Damped Spring-Mass System ▼

Problem: A damped spring-mass system is modeled by the differential equation \(\ddot{x} + 0.5\dot{x} + 2x = 0\). Convert to a first-order system and find its eigenvalues.

Solution:

Let \(x_1 = x\) and \(x_2 = \dot{x}\). Then \(\dot{x}_1 = x_2\) and \(\dot{x}_2 = -2x_1 - 0.5x_2\).

The system matrix is:

\[A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -2 & -0.5 \end{bmatrix}\]

Characteristic polynomial: \(\lambda^2 + 0.5\lambda + 2 = 0\)

\[\lambda = \frac{-0.5 \pm \sqrt{0.25 - 8}}{2} = \frac{-0.5 \pm \sqrt{-7.75}}{2} = -0.25 \pm 1.391i\]

Modulus: \(r = \sqrt{0.0625 + 1.936} \approx 1.414 > 1\)... wait, let me recalculate.

\[r = \sqrt{(-0.25)^2 + 1.391^2} = \sqrt{0.0625 + 1.936} \approx 1.41\]

Actually for stability, we need the continuous case: damping means the real part is negative.

Answer: \(\lambda = -0.25 \pm 1.391i\). The negative real part indicates the system is stable and oscillates with frequency \(\approx 1.391\) and decay rate 0.25.

Example 5.5 (Hard): Coupled Rotations in Robotics ▼

Problem: A 2-joint robot arm is modeled by \(\mathbf{x}_{k+1} = A\mathbf{x}_k\) where \(A = \begin{bmatrix} \cos(15°) & -\sin(15°) \\ \sin(15°) & \cos(15°) \end{bmatrix}\). Find the eigenvalues and interpret the motion.

Solution:

This is a pure rotation matrix by 15°. For a rotation matrix \(R_\theta = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix}\), the eigenvalues are \(\lambda = \cos\theta \pm i\sin\theta = e^{\pm i\theta}\).

Therefore: \(\lambda = \cos(15°) \pm i\sin(15°) = e^{\pm i\pi/12}\)

Numerically: \(\lambda \approx 0.9659 \pm 0.2588i\)

Modulus: \(r = 1\) (no scaling, pure rotation)

Argument: \(\theta = 15° = \pi/12\)

Answer: Each iteration rotates the state vector by 15° without scaling. After 24 iterations, the state returns to its original direction (24 × 15° = 360°).
